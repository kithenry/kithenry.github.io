On Transitioning to Analog

E023 | 03.05.25

Feeling Overwhelmed and Reflecting on Stress

About three weeks ago, even though I wasn’t working a 9-5 job, I felt on the verge of breaking down. I had this constant sensation of facing 100 deadlines with only three days to meet them. I wondered why others, who seemed to have heavier workloads, didn’t appear as stressed. It hit me that I had become the kind of person I dislike associating with—someone always complaining about how rough life is, implying their workload is the heaviest on the planet and selfishly suggesting everyone else’s problems aren’t significant.

I tried to find a solution, questioning if I truly had too many tasks. As you can probably deduce, that wasn’t the case. Was it my diet, my sleep, or the time I spent on my phone? I remembered how hooked I was on YouTube Shorts. I hated how they stripped me of my will to stop after watching just one video, yet I returned daily.

Digital Habits and Self-Resentment

WhatsApp was another issue. I had overcome impulsively posting status updates, but I’d gradually fallen into binge-watching others’ statuses. I’d hang around in WhatsApp, answering a text or two, swiping up and down… just because. I felt many of my internet decisions fueled a boiling resentment toward myself. I knew these habits were damaging, but not enough to stop.

That feeling after watching 100 shorts on YouTube, TikTok, or any platform—where your brain feels torn apart, like you’re without control—I felt that in smaller doses from all these apps. Combined, they messed with my mental clarity. I couldn’t focus.

Music Addiction and Constant Stimulation

Then there was music. I have several Spotify playlists curated for different moods and occasions, and that curation alone was, I think, a full-blown addiction. I loved the songs—all of them—and still do. I couldn’t go anywhere without my earphones plugged in. I’d recently bought earphones with such great battery life and a sleek design that I could wear them 24 hours a day if I didn’t need to shower.

I’d read about how listening to music while reading affects concentration and retention, but that wasn’t enough to outweigh the enjoyment I got from it. I also had overstimulation problems, but that’s beside the point.

The Breaking Point and Going Analog

Three weeks ago, while at “work” trying to tweak my phone to make it less distracting—failing to lock it away in some no-distraction Faraday cage—I said, “Enough is enough.” I dashed to my room like a madman, dropped my phone there, and returned with a pen and notebook.

Since November 2024, I’d been quietly wanting to do this but finally got fed up enough to act. I’d been buying notebooks, small calculators, even a functional pocket watch for this purpose. The transition wasn’t so bad; much of it had already happened in my head.

Benefits of the Analog Life

It’s been about 21 days living this way—not perfectly consistent, as you might notice if you track when I’m online, but I’ve reaped significant benefits nonetheless. A while back, I experienced an after-sleep feeling that triggered nostalgia, reminiscent of the refreshed feeling I had after 10 days of morning runs (thanks to David Goggins’ motivation).

I’ve always gotten anxious about exams. My brain has frozen multiple times during tests, causing me to fail even papers I was fully prepared for. I’m not saying I got straight A’s on all my recent papers—that’s not the point. A few days ago, during exams, I noticed something interesting: I was calm for all the papers, whether I’d prepared or not. It wasn’t an “I don’t care” attitude, but I approached them like ungraded assignments, did what I needed to do, and left without ruminating. This was a complete contrast to before my analog move.

Linking Smartphones to Stress

There could be many reasons for this change beyond going analog, but it was surprising to learn that smartphone usage is linked to academic stress and anxiety, according to research. It also affects sleep quality and more. Days now feel longer and less boring. Since I ditched my phone, I also ditched earphones and music. That meant listening to birds while walking to class and concentrating without stimulants while reading.

Rejecting Consumerism

I’ve long been a proponent of functionality over “bling bling,” if that means anything. I value things for their purpose in my life, not because they’re the newest version or will earn me a seat among the big boys. I’d been postponing buying a phone I thought would be my last purchase—one I’d keep for a long time. Now, I no longer feel the need to get a new phone. Why should I?

By breaking down my device into its components, I’ve learned there’s more efficiency than loss in doing so. The Swiss Army knife of a smartphone promises productivity but, for the most part, takes it away—along with the life of the phone's owner. I’ve also noticed a potential link between prolonged smartphone dependence and defining one’s identity through them, at least financially. It makes sense if you think about it.

Moving Forward and Encouraging Others

I’m not yet a full “monk analog,” but I’m gradually shifting that way. I’m not a Luddite; I just want to use what I need. Stress, ditching my phone, sleeping better, managing stress better, feeling less pulled into consumerism, and improved concentration—there’s more to discover.

Maybe I’m just overly stimulated, as I hinted earlier, but you might want to try this too. There are many documented benefits, and I’d like to build on them in the future.
