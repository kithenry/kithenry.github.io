All or Nothing, Always nothing

A lot has been written about perfectionism and how limiting it can be to growth. That need to build great things the non conventional way.

Conventionally , you want to start with anything then , as you learn from gradually fixing the mistakes with your first draft, build that great thing maybe by draft 50 or 100. But sure thing is, draft N will almost always be (if you are consciously learning from the journey) greater than draft N-1 . So there's from draft one a guarantee you'll reap feelings of accomplishment. Your confidence , real confidence not ego I mean, will growing simultaneously also. And who knows what the limit is to improvement, there's none for what we know. (well, there's sweet spots and all that but , please let me make my point ;)

Now for the perfectionist (which I struggle everyday not to be, which you should not be), the process is something like; They want draft infinity (that is, the limit I just opined we could not percieve what it is). They want draft one out with all the mistakes fixed. And they want to be able to maintain it that way.

So the perfectionist doesn't know what the mistakes will be, but they just want them fixed. They lack the skills to build the most flawed version. The baseline for initial improvement. But they want to build what's improved to the maximum*. How about we integrate to the first draft. Oh, wait they don't do gradual drafting, there's only draft 0... People take months, some years of failing and failing again to build this, and they want to speak years of work into existence like.. pff .. no mistakes whatsoever .. just like that? Draft 0? That's like demi-god tier stuff.

Perfectionists don’t start with draft 0, the messy first attempt we all need. They want draft infinity—perfect, flawless, ultimate improvement—right at draft 0. It’s like expecting years of expertise to materialize instantly, no mistakes allowed.

For a lack of words, if stated that way, it puts under a magnifying glass the irrationality that underlies perfectionism. I don't think its the place for it nor do I think myself to be the right person to talk about all the underlying psychology behind this. Quite a number of articles on that online already.

But, how does one expect to build perfection without the expertise to build the most imperfect draft. Think about that when you feel like skipping the basics, like really.

Now here's the problems with this. The perfect first draft always fails eventually. Mostly due to the lack of skill to maintain it. And that's around the same time when the perfectionist's big baloon of ego pops after a long time swelling. Even if they are lucky to come up with something that's decently good. The wind of time eventually blows away the masked insufficiencies that surely underlie the facade of expertise that's their work. And it's sad how most go back and re-iterate the same unproductive loop.

“Rare image of a perfectionist chasing after perfection: a hamster spinning furiously in a wheel, aiming for draft infinity’s perfection while stuck at draft 0.

Okays, now, what to do about it? Here's what I meditate on to snap myself back into reality.

Why you don't have to be perfect

  • You ain't perfect
    • And no one is. Every human being can become better, they ain't the best version of themselves. Thus, every human being is imperfect (I am not saying God's work is imperfect, that's a huge religious conversation we can't have from on here).
    • There's going to be imperfections in every human being's work. Depending on how improved they are. And that's normal. Good actually. What would we do if all we had in the world was the best it could be? There would be no more problems to solve would there? How about the fun there is adding that next new improvement to your work. Same thing.
  • There's lessons to be learnt from failure, they are how you grow.
    • With every attempt, there's two possible outcomes -> a success or a fail.
    • What's good here? You know what does not work. That's less mistakes to make come the next attempt. Learning is failing till you fail less and less and less.
    • Thus, take away failure, take away learning also.
  • True confidence is built upon experience. Most of experience is the failures the perfectionist avoids
    • And confidence built on zero experience is EGO. That's ugly. You don't want to be egotistical but truly confident I think.
  • Perfection is unattainable, and that's a good thing
    • No improvement possible with perfection. That's bad.

Are you God? Right, I knew you weren't. Be humble and start with the basics.
