On why learning feels hard, till it isn't

E025 | 05.05.25

Intro: Lamenting mental strain

I was thinking about how much easier my life could become if I didn't have to go through so much mental-strain to learn new things. I mean, I think I have, like all else, mastered quite an amount of subject topics and doing them to me, is as easy as 1+1. But it's like I wasn't paying much attention to how much effort it took me to get myself from level to level for the different levels of mastery I am at in various life domains.

The Dip: Almost quit

Now this thought was because I am learning quite a number of things currently and the initial learning process for some of those is so mentally involved. This mentally involved stage is taking so long I noticed I was starting to feel like for some of these learning ventures, it was going to be the reason why I quit.

Mental Strain Tax: The price of leveling up

But then thinking about it I was like , I mean I am sure you've heard some form of this before, that mental strain is the price we have to pay for growth in expertise. That crossing from one skill level to the next was always going to have an initial stage of strain, which would eventually due to familiarity from daily exposure, challenges, and feedback, become the learner's next defition of easy. That the length of the strain was going to be dependednt on a lot of factors, but all kept constant, the subject matter's difficulty and my effort to make it easy.

Common grounds: There's nothing new here

But then I was like, why am I acting like I was born last night? This is not new at all. The learning strain in academics I meant. I was like most of learning was fundamentally the same at the core and that, I knew this already. That it was a series of strainous exercises of memorizing patterns and building muscle memory for a simplistic definition. Which I think is what was happening every time I, for a piano piece, a few minutes before playing it so well I was impressed, thought it was going to be why I quit playing the instrument.

The Expert’s Secret: Strain as a compass

Noticing this parallel made me relate the principle to all fields in which I had gained some level of skill. And confirmingly, I noticed it held false for none. There was the common pattern: relative strain in the beginning, a perception of ease, strain again during a cross over to the next skill level.

All this thinking is probably how I got myself back up to speed in my race chasing the next level of expertise.
