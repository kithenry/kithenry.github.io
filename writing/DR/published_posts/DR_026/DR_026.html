On what is within my circle of control

Inspiration for this was from the Jan 13th entry of Ryan Holiday's The Daily Stoic.

The quote was from Epictetus' Discourses and it went something like;

We control our reasoned choice and all acts that depend on that moral will. What's not under our control are the body and any of its parts, our possessions, parents, siblings, children, or country -- anything with which we might associate.

This got me thinking about what I am getting myself to often lately. My circle of control. Refining my definition of it. It's interesting how small it keeps getting. A lot of things over which I obsess I learn everyday are, nothing but energy sinks. For which reason I prune them then from the inner circle. Now I didn't take it there and then that my body too, or my possessions, like Epictetus mentioned, had to be pruned. But then that was probably due to how I had interpreted his statement.

I was like, well, if I didn't eat right, then, whose fault was it going it be, my body's possible demise? ** Mine right? Or if I didn't make sure my possessions were protected, then on whom was the loss of going to be? I found the answer to that to have been an easy ** Me. For parents or siblings , children and country, I felt like I'd had it already mastered they were not within my hands. Underlined words, felt.

But then from a different perspective, I learnt I actually still had all that the quote mentioned if not more, included under my circle of control. See, I think I was right about my thought that not doing what I could for what possessions I had (my body included) would++ have meant me neglecting what was within my control. That It would++ have had me responsible for the consequences that would++ have ensued from the neglect of my personal responsibility.

I was however wrong about one crucial thing; The actual definition of what was within my control. Was it within my control, the state I meant to put my possessions under by doing what I could, or was it doing what I could that was within my control? I realized that's where my confusion was.

I was like, I knew I could do a lot towards ends of ** making sure I was healthy. I could manage my sleep, make sure I worked out often, ate healthy and all that good stuff. But if while I was out on a run I got bitten by a stray cobra, who'd have taken the blame for my death? If after I'd ensured my home's was ** secure from ** all kinds of physical threat, or thought so after I'd installed ** all kinds of security measures, who'd I have blamed in the event it got hit by a nuclear missile?

Thinking about this made me more conscious of the flawed logic I was using to define my circle of control. It dawned on me that even what I thought was within my control, was not fully within my control. I mentioned I felt I'd mastered it that humans, and the country's state of affairs was no longer on the list of things to be concerned about. Well, how about NO, I was lying to myself.

I mean, if I still felt bad that after trying all I could to do what would** make them the happy parents I wanted them to be, they weren't as happy as I expected they would, then wasn't I trying to control their happiness? Wasn't I trying to control the uncontrollable? If I had my happiness so predicated on **what my friends thought of me and tried to please them by for that reason, wasn't I trying to control another variable that was outside my circle of control; My friends happiness?

So what's within my control? My choice to do what I can do, and all actions dependent upon that. The effect of my actions however well executed, like I had gotten myself to believe even without knowing, wasn't in my hands.

I noticed how this thought I had more control than I actually did permeated even my language. Notice where there's asterisks. The assumption of full control, where there's a lot of other variables at play. In an attempt to realign my mindset, I started off by replacing all the asterisks ( **) with a possibly and all the would++'s with a could.

I concluded that attempting to the best of my abilities to ensure success, whichever definition it had for any given context, was my responsibility. I added to that the concluding thought that, after making the best possible attempt, I'd successfully done my part. That what came after, whether success or failure, was as outside my hands as the sun's setting direction. And the simplicity of that felt relieving.
