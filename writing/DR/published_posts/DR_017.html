Work expands to fill up the time given for its completion

E017 | 27.04.25

Work expands to fill up the time it's given for completion.

That's right. It's Perkinson's law. You know how you decide you won't be unrealistically ambitious for some day so you choose you'll only have two main tasks done after which, anything that will come after will be extra?

You know how often it is, I mean it is for me, that you end up doing these tasks at the very end of the day if not postponding them to the next.

Now contrast this with a day when you have a lot on your plate. It seems to me you , even if the tasks , each require an equal amount of effort as those from the two task list, would check more off the list before the day's end all other factors kept constant. Worse if they are all nearing their deadlines.

Why is this so?

I think this is in some sense related to procrastination. Shifting towards the deadline work we know we can get done with in a short while if we start right now only to compress the time it takes to complete it to what's left towards the deadline.

Prepping the work environment. Creating some errands and working on those to give you a false feeling of busyness. Doing all sorts of preparation and planning work. Till you have no more time and now you scream.. "Oh, I don't have any time time left. Let's get this done"

It get's even more interesting when you read the original article. It has something to do with beauracracies and all the sort. But that's the original article. I mean this one. Theories about how group tasks fuel procrastination and things related.

--- Now solution time , or experiment time

I think if Perkinson's law is that related to procrastination then the same solutions to procrastination would apply to it.

All the solutions listed in the book 'Eat That Frog'. Here's a short list;

  • Always have a list of things you need to do written down
  • Do only and only and only what you have planned you will do first , the rest can come later
    • And this will save you from doing anything and everything but not what you have to be doing.
  • Your plan should be written in stone
    • If it's 3 tasks a day, that's it. No compromises. Just so you focus. Also makes it so you always feel accomplished after your list is all crossed out.
  • Always start with the worst task, the task you are most likely to procrastinate on, or -- 'Eat the ugliest frog first'
    • It's usually the most important task of the day that we procrastinate on the most
  • Be super clear when writing what tasks need to be done.
    • Vagueness fuels procrastination which leads to rushing towards deadlines

Here's one I haven't tried out yet that I hope to return with the results on how it faired when I do. How about because I tend to stretch how much time a task can actually take to how much time I have given it, I give a rough estimate of what it would really take (before assigning it a time slot that is), then I give it a time slot that is tigheter.

Now the problem will obviously be knowing how much time you can take to get some particular task done. Which I will also try to solve. Time myself for most of the major tasks and slowly get more and more accurate results regards the feasibility of this solution.
