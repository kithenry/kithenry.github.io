Focus, systems, goals

E020 | 30.04.25

So I was reflecting on what caused my anxiety towards being in situations where my performance would possibly not get me the results I wanted. Something I realized spanned across exam preparation, intern applications and all other such situations. Why failure got so much to my head, as equally as success did. And why it was hard for me to win again after I had, but easy for me to get back into the unhealthy cycle of anxiety towards results.

This reminded me of something I read from James Clear's Atomic Habits. In one chapter he talks about some basket ball team which for its obsession with the score was failing over and over, but after it started doing things differently, it went on a winning spree that lasted quite some time. [article at the bottom]

And that is where I found my answer. It was interesting relating this to my own life. Attempting to solve my own anxiety and inaction problems. So I learnt that there was a number of possible reasons for my anxiety. Or one that led to all the others, which made them all interconnected in some sense. anxiety, inaction, erratic performance, lack of growth or slow growth, more anxiety all caused by anxiety.

Over and over again, I have tried to teach myself to care only about things over which I have control but the lessons never seem to stick. I am always going to catch myself obsessing over if it'll rain tomorrow, or if the bird that wakes up first the next day will be a crow. Yeah, such things as if the HR guys will consider my application or if the paper will be easy enough for me to pass.

It was thinking about that recently and this thinking made me conscious of what effects it had on me. Anxiety. The feeling an article from Big-think says humans have when in situations of uncertainty, and thus situations where they are powerless over what will happen. Effects from total inaction due to fear things might not go as I want them to, half-hearted preparation, to inefficient or haphazard action in attempts to hit the goal when action time came.

It looked like my fears for possible failure, due to focusing on the goal other than the steps that led to it, almost always became a self fulfilling prophecy. Exams for instance, the cycle was like:

I am afraid I won't make it --> why should I prepare, I won't make it --> it's time to try, but will I make it?, oh, this is really hard, I might not make it. Time is not enough, I am not prepared, I am done, i won't make it --> results are not yet out, but will i make it? i really pray that I make it --> Failure / Success. But most often; failure

If the result was failure --> I knew i would fail --> cycle repeated itself. And if it was success --> Oh, I am lucky I made it --> this is easy --> next attempt --> failure --> cycle repeats itself.

I realized my obsession with the goal was why I never got to grow from any failure, I never reflected on how well my attempts were executed. Goal was the goal, not the process. Why my success was defined by if an attempt was successful not if I made my best attempt whenever I had the chance to.

Why I never developed a system that I kept on improving with every other attempt because I was never about systems, only about the goal. Why if I won, it was hard to win again, I was anxious I would fail come next attempt which most times I did, because the last win had resulted arbitrary unplanned moves or -- sheer luck.

Why if I failed, it really got to my head. It was some sort of confirming definition for what I already thought I was capable of -- nothing. Because it not about gradual improvement but winning the first time or being a loser.

Systems. Systems. Systems. The fruit of all this thinking was a stronger belief in the mindset I was already trying to cultivate in myself. One I am still in the process of making a part of every aspect of my life. A systems first mindset. A mindset that defines success as making the best possible attempt and making an attempt that's any percentage better come next chance to do so. One where the path to the goal was clearly marked and hitting it was not so much lack but very much planning and strategy.

One where there was not much anxiety, because the causes of that, uncertainty, were absent. Since its success was not defined by hitting the goal (something over which it had not control), but becoming more capable of hitting the goal, something that was fully in its hands.

I think this line of thought was inspired so much by the fact that I have read Atomic Habits quite a number of times. But anyways.
