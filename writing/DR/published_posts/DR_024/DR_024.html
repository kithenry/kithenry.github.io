On overcoming the self sabotage of task vagueness

E024 | 04.03.25

Vagueness

I’ve been reflecting on the dissatisfaction I have been feeling at the end of some days, even when I’ve checked off every item on my to-do list. For certain tasks marked as "done," I was feeling like they weren’t truly complete. I recalled a point from Eat That Frog by Brian Tracy, where he discusses how vagueness fuels procrastination. Vaguely defined tasks allow the brain to escape doing the real work, as we can stretch the definition of "done" to include superficial actions and still call the day successful.

Resentment

I realized this vagueness might be why I was feeling resentful. For tasks where I know exactly what needs to be done but feel afraid to tackle, I noticed I'd made it a habit to broaden the range of acceptable actions to include easier ones that would give quick dopamine hits. It’s like a child who, when told to clean the house, mops just one room because it technically counts as cleaning. The resentment, I think, stemmed from knowing I was lying to myself. By trying to celebrate a day as successful based on tasks where I avoided the real work, I created some sort of cognitive dissonance. It’s interesting how the human mind has its own way of sending corrective signals when we’re at odds with the truth.

Solution

Anyway, to address this, I decided every item on my to-do list would have to be as specific as possible. No need for perfectionism though. For example, I replaced a task like "attend class" with "attend all lectures," then refined it to "be active in all lectures," and finally to "ask at least one question in each lecture."

For someone building a reading habit, a vague task like "read something" might become "read book X," then "read two pages of book X," and ultimately "summarize in your own words what you learned from reading the first two pages of book X."

I wasn’t overly concerned with when I completed these tasks, though I recognize time management could be a separate issue, possibly addressed by James Clear’s Implementation Intentions. The problem I was solving was task’s definition vagueness. Ensuring each task included actions that moved me quickly toward my goal. That nstead of grabbing a quote from Instagram and checking off "read something" (which I’d know wasn’t the reading I'd have needed for a reading habit), I'd specify summarizing in my own words what I learned from two pages of a chosen book, X. This would require comprehension and ensure concrete progress.

Conclusion

With tasks defined this clearly, failing to complete them I was sure could only result from a lack of discipline or -- stubbornly self sabotaging. The specificity I think left no room for ambiguity or self-deception.
