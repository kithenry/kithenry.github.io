On overcoming the self sabotage of task vagueness

E024 | 04.03.25

Reflections on Task Specificity and Procrastination Vagueness I’ve been reflecting on the dissatisfaction I feel at the end of some days, even when I’ve checked off every item on my to-do list. For certain tasks marked as "done," I still felt they weren’t truly complete. I recalled a point from Eat That Frog by Brian Tracy, where he discusses how vagueness fuels procrastination. Vaguely defined tasks allow the brain to escape doing the real work, as we can stretch the definition of "done" to include superficial actions and still call the day successful.

Resentment I realized this vagueness might be why I was feeling resentful. For tasks where I know exactly what needs to be done but feel afraid to tackle, I tend to broaden the range of acceptable actions to include easier ones that give quick dopamine hits. It’s like a child who, when told to clean the house, mops just one room because it technically counts as cleaning. The resentment, I think, stems from knowing I’m lying to myself. By trying to celebrate a day as successful based on tasks where I avoided the real work, I created cognitive dissonance. It’s fascinating how the human mind sends signals when we’re at odds with the truth.

Solution To address this, I decided every item on my to-do list should be as specific as possible. For example, I replaced "attend class" with "attend all lectures," then refined it to "be active in all lectures," and finally to "ask at least one question in each lecture."

For someone building a reading habit, a vague task like "read something" might become "read book X," then "read two pages of book X," and ultimately "summarize in your own words what you learned from reading the first two pages of book X."

I wasn’t overly concerned with when I completed these tasks, though I recognize time management could be a separate issue, possibly addressed by James Clear’s Implementation Intentions. The key was ensuring each task’s definition included actions that moved me quickly toward my goal. Instead of grabbing a quote from Instagram and checking off "read something" (which I’d know wasn’t the reading needed for a reading habit), I specified summarizing in my own words what I learned from two pages of a chosen book, X. This requires comprehension and ensures concrete progress.

Conclusion With tasks defined this clearly, failing to complete them could only result from a lack of discipline—stubbornly sabotaging myself. The specificity leaves no room for ambiguity or self-deception.
