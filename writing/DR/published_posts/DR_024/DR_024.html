On overcoming the self sabotage of task vagueness

E024 | 04.05.25

Vagueness

I’ve been reflecting on the dissatisfaction I have been feeling at the end of some days, even when I’d checked off every item on my to-do list. For certain tasks marked as "done," I was having this feeling they weren’t truly complete. Brian Tracy, in Eat That Frog mentions that vagueness fuels procrastination. Vaguely defined tasks allow the brain to escape doing the real work, as we can stretch the definition of "done" to include superficial actions and still call the day successful.

Resentment

I was like this vagueness might be why I was feeling resentful. For tasks where I knew exactly what needed to be done but felt afraid to tackle, I noticed I'd made it a habit to broaden the range of acceptable actions to include easier ones that would give quick dopamine hits. I was like that kid who, when told to clean the house, mops just one room because; that's cleaning the house also. The resentment, I think, stemmed from knowing I was lying to myself. By trying to celebrate a day as successful based on tasks where I avoided the real work, I created some sort of cognitive dissonance within. I find it interesting how the human system has its own way of sending signals for us to realign when we’re at odds with the truth.

Solution

Anyway, to address this, I was like every item on my to-do list would have to be as specific as possible (not so much, only specific enough). For example, I replaced tasks like "attend class" with "attend all lectures," then refined that to "be active in all lectures," and finally to "ask at least one question in each lecture."

For someone building a reading habit, a vague task like "read something" might become something like "read book X," then turn to "read two pages of book X," and ultimately settle at "summarize in your own words what you learned from reading the first two pages of book X."

I wasn’t overly concerned with when I completed these tasks, though I recognize time management could be a separate issue, possibly addressed by James Clear’s Implementation Intentions. The problem I was solving was task’s definition vagueness. Ensuring each task included actions that moved me quickly toward the goal for which it was created. That nstead of grabbing a quote from Instagram and checking off "read something" (which I’d know wasn’t the reading I'd have needed for a reading habit), I'd specify summarizing in my own words what I learned from two pages of a chosen book, X. This description would need comprehension and would ensure actual progress.

Conclusion

With tasks defined this clearly, failing to complete them I was sure was only going to result from sheer refusal. The specificity I think left no room for ambiguity or self-deception.
